A trio of Texan expatriates traded in their corporate lives in America to move into the jungle and build an eco-lodge on the secluded paradise of the privately-owned Tranquilo Bay in Bocas del Toro.

Written by: Anne Marie Haggerty

Photos and Video by: Paris Alston and Tegan Johnston

Graphic by: Cori Dymond and Caroline McKinley

A wooden dock peeks out from a collection of sculpted mangroves. Its long walkway invites guests to enter the secluded splendor of Tranquilo Bay, an eco-adventure lodge.

To get to the archipelago, travelers sail across Caribbean waters from the mainland of Bocas del Toro to a peninsula called Bastimentos. This peninsula is home to Tranquilo Bay.

A green tunnel, carved out of interlacing roots that rise from the water welcomes explorers. Travellers ascend a dark wooden staircase to find high ceilings dotted with creamy palm-blade fans circulating sultry air as it drifts through the wrapped balcony. Handcrafted adirondack chairs allow guests to sit back and take in the panorama of leafy treetops and distant water visible from the terrace.

Winding back down through the flora and fauna, travelers arrive at stucco-style cabanas adorned with leisurely hammocks. Guests can then choose to meet fish through a snorkeling mask in the coral reef, kayak in the aquamarine sea, explore bat caves, search for new species of butterflies in the tropical jungle or meet lazy sloths hanging in the trees.

But before the haven of Tranquilo Bay was built, it was just a large expanse of wild, untamed land. A team of Texans from corporate America with no experience in construction or entrepreneurship swapped their lives in the United States for a new home in the depths of the jungle.

Taming a Wild Paradise

Jim and Renee Kimball are a spirited Texan couple. They met in college and agreed on a mutual dream of one day owning their own business in the tropics.

“When I met Jim, we agreed we never wanted to go the typical path,” Renee Kimball said. The couple shared this vision with a close college friend, Jay Viola, who agreed to join them.

The trio aspired to create a haven in the tropics that would give vacationers the chance to enjoy nature’s beauty in an environmentally responsible way. When the team found the peaceful, lush 200-acre property in Bastimentos in 1999, they knew this was the place where they would make their dream a reality. It was where they would build Tranquilo Bay. The Texans promised to develop and protect the land by creating a destination for adventurous eco-tourists to snorkel and hike through multiple island beaches to enjoy the captivating, tropical beauty of Panama while maintaining a healthy respecting the environment.

For this dream to become a reality, Jim Kimball and Jay Viola left their life in Texas and moved into the depths of the Panamanian jungle with machetes in hand to tame the wild jungle for symbiotic use. Neither of the two had formal training in construction and couldn’t find a reliable contractor in the remote area, so they searched the internet for plans on how to construct electric, plumbing and HVAC systems. Renee Kimball stayed in Texas working 15-hour days as a technology attorney to send funds to the two novice craftsmen. After five long years, construction was complete, and Renee happily resigned from her corporate job to reunite with the team in Panama to officially open Tranquilo Bay.

The Future of Paradise

The Bastimentos ecolodge lures travelers who crave a reprieve from the demands of daily life and want to enjoy the unadulterated beauty of nature.

Thousands of tourists flock to Panama each year to enjoy the tropical scenery, which is beneficial for the economy in Panama. The service industry makes up for 75 percent of the Panamanian economy -- tourism is a significant part of that industry.

Recently, tourism has been the subject of much debate on the political scene. Vice President Isabel San Malo and mirror Endara de Heras, minister of the Environmental Authority of Panama, came to Bocas del Toro in March to hold a meeting that Renee Kimball, among other business owners in the area, was invited to attend.

Heras spoke about the need for sustainable methods of tourism in order to keep the industry alive. The concern is that unsustainable, exploitative tourism will adversely impact the environment to the point that it is no longer beautiful and cannot attract tourists.

The United Nations Division on Development addressed this issue in the third edition of its Indicators and Sustainable Development guidelines. According to the guidelines, “The use of natural resources by local residents takes a toll on the land, and development is often bad for the environment.”

While development and disruption of the land can have detrimental effects, places like Tranquilo Bay are working to lessen this impact by providing sustainable options like wildlife photography, tarpon fishing, kayaking, Panama birding and surfing for eco-travelers to enjoy without a deleterious carbon footprint.

The mission of sustainable development, according to the United Nations, is to meet present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs. For Jim and Renee Kimball, this mission is personal. Jim and Renee’s children, Tres and Boty, and Jay’s sons Scott and Patrick, are the generation of the future, and the mission of Tranquilo Bay is to care for the land in such a way allows their children to one day do the same.
