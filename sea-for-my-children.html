The Caribbean Sea is flat. Occasionally, a wave erupts and distorts the peace, but on a still, cloudy day, watching the sea from a swaying boat, it doesn’t change much.

Under the sea, however, the quickest environmental change since the Jurassic Period 145 million years ago is challenging the coral reefs of Bocas del Toro, Panama, to adapt and survive, or die altogether.

Bocas del Toro’s corals suffer from harmful conditions due to extreme rises in the Caribbean Sea’s temperatures, Karl Castillo and Janina Seemann agreed. Castillo is a marine ecologist at UNC-Chapel Hill and and Seeman is a marine ecologist at the Smithsonian Tropical Research Institute in Bocas del Toro. Because water temperatures are rising in the region, the marine ecosystem largely supported by coral reefs is rapidly deteriorating.

Corals are animals similar to anemone: they move very little — only enough to pluck a small percentage of their food out of the water with their tentacles — so they rely on a symbiotic relationship with algae to obtain energy. Algae is a plant that gains energy through photosynthesis, which converts carbon dioxide into oxygen, and the oxygen is then fed to the algae’s host coral.

Recently, chemicals that fight against CO2 called oxidants have contaminated the seawater around Bocas del Toro and prevented algae from feeding themselves or their coral properly.

As with all life, when energy intake stops, so does the ability to survive. As the surrounding algae begin to die, the coral eventually loses its color and primary food source, undergoing what is called bleaching. What’s left of the coral is its outer white skeleton.

Coral bleaching signifies that a coral reef will soon die. So After this began occurring on a large scale in the 1980s and 1990s, Seemann and other marine ecologists who study coral ecosystems, such as Karl Castillo, took to their research labs and began to understand what caused the bleaching and how the environment could begin to improve.

"These might be the next powerhouses of the Caribbean."

In Chapel Hill, North Carolina, Karl Castillo and his lab of marine ecologists could be on the brink of identifying corals strong enough to overcome the extreme Caribbean environment.

“I don’t like to use the word global warming.”

“Some of the coral are pretty bad-ass,” said one researcher in the lab about Siderastrea siderea, commonly known as the massive starlet coral and affectionately called ‘Big Red’ by local Smithsonian researchers. The coral species has proven its ability to withstand the rising temperatures of its natural habitat in the Caribbean Sea.

Over the past year, the Castillo Lab extracted samples of the massive starlet coral from Bocas del Toro and flew them to UNC-Chapel Hill’s marine biology labs to examine how they react to certain environmental changes.

The lab rigged water tanks and light bulbs to mimic natural sea and sunlight conditions to study some of the samples. The researchers said a coral’s ideal temperature is 28 C, and it will often die if that temperature changes more than one or two degrees. But they found the massive starlet coral was able to survive in a 32 C environment where all other conditions were nearly identical to its natural state in the Caribbean Sea.

Kathryn Cobleigh, the lab’s technician and a 2015 graduate of UNC-Chapel Hill, studied the coral as an undergraduate researcher in the lab. “These might be the next powerhouses of the Caribbean,” she said.

"Nature is part of you. You are a part of the nature."

Some say the reason the sea’s complex ecosystem is changing so drastically is caused partly by the community of people surrounding it. Although people indigenous to the island have an ancestral attachment to caring for the land and sea around them, many islanders and tourists don’t understand the long-term effects of the dying coral reef.

In March, Panamanian Minister of the Environment Mirei Endara de Heras held a press conference in Bocas del Toro to discuss opportunities for government-regulated land preservation in the region.

“People don’t understand the importance of protecting the land,” said Samuel Valdés Díaz, director of protected areas and wildlife for the Panamanian Ministry of the Environment.

To mitigate some of that misunderstanding, Seemann works to educate her community on the island in addition to her work with the Smithsonian Institution.

Several years ago, she visited a local high school to teach students about the history of the coral reefs around their island. She instructed the students to interview their grandparents about what the coral reef was like when they were younger.

She said students better understood the coral’s change from the project than by simply talking about it. But a more recent visit to a school in Bocas del Toro showed that although ninth grade students learn an appreciation for recycling and environmental health, many don’t know the cause of the dying corals and how it could hurt their futures.

Although it may be difficult to sustain coral-health education in schools, Seemann teaches her children to be mindful of the world around them.

“We educated them about cycles – how nothing disappears. Nature is part of you. You are a part of the nature,” Seemann said. “And then also, give them a solution.”
